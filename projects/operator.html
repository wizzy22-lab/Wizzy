Overview

Project Summary

Small business owners need to decide — not just adjust

Right now, HVAC systems give small business owners buttons and settings. But they don't tell them what to do with them.

They want to keep the store comfortable and manage electricity costs at the same time. Without knowing which action leads to which result, they guess. And they keep guessing, over and over.

This project started from my store operation experience and my teammate's analysis of how electricity costs actually work.

Define

The Problem Is Not Missing Controls — It's Missing Criteria

01

Problem Definition

This is not about adding more controls. Small business owners already have controls. The problem is that they don't know when to use them, what to base their decisions on, or what will happen after. That's the gap this project addresses.

Insight

The core issue is not missing features — it's missing a structure for deciding

02

Root Cause Analysis

Why do small business owners keep guessing? Because the system never shows results. When they change the temperature or mode, they don't see how comfort will shift, or how much cost will go up. Without that, every decision is a gamble.

Insight

No feedback loop — no learning — same mistakes over and over

03

Persona & User Journey

The busier the store, the less time small business owners have to think. When things get crowded, they need to make HVAC decisions fast — with no time to analyze. So they go with intuition. The persona work showed this is not a skill problem. It's a time + criteria problem.

Insight

Small business owners don't make bad decisions — they make decisions without the right information at the right time

Ideate

Show What to Do Now — and What Will Happen

01

Solution Direction

The answer is not more data. Small business owners already feel overwhelmed by information they can't use. The answer is showing the right action and the expected result, at the right moment.

Core

Not more information — decision clarity at the right moment

02

Functional Structuring

We mapped out all the variables that affect HVAC decisions: weather, time of day, store occupancy, electricity rate. Then we connected each variable to a specific function — so the system could give small business owners the right information at the right moment.

Insight

Every decision involves multiple variables — the system must reflect that, simply

Functional Structure operator/ideate/functional-structure.jpg
03

Scenario & Prioritization

We designed features around real decision moments — when the store gets crowded, when outside temperature spikes, when the electricity rate peaks. Features were mapped to these moments, then prioritized using a 2×2 matrix and MoSCoW to define the MVP.

Insight

It's not about having the right features — it's about having them at the right moment

04

Ideation & User Flow Design

The flow is designed to match how small business owners think, not how HVAC systems are built. Instead of showing data and asking them to figure things out, the flow guides: here's the situation → here's what you can do → here's what will happen.

Insight

The key is not the amount of information — it's the order

05

IA & System Design

The decision flow — understand → choose → act → see result — became the structure for the IA and system logic. Every screen, every interaction was designed to keep that flow intact.

Insight

Good structure makes decisions feel effortless

The first version revealed two structural issues — both about flow. Places where small business owners had to break their thinking to navigate the interface.

Reflection

Reflection

What Designing for Decisions Taught Me

01

Reframing the Problem Is the First Design Decision

I started thinking about HVAC control. But the real problem was something else — small business owners had no way to make decisions. Changing that framing changed everything: what we designed, what we prioritized, and what we left out.

02

Data Is Only Useful If It Helps Someone Decide

Small business owners had access to data. But they couldn't use it. The problem wasn't missing data — it was that nothing translated the data into a decision. That's the gap UX should fill.

03

Good UX Supports the Decision, Not Just the Action

Most systems are designed to help users do things. But small business owners didn't struggle with doing — they struggled with knowing what to do. That distinction shaped every design choice we made.

04

Structure Matters More Than Features

We could have added more features. We chose not to. A clear flow that small business owners can follow is worth more than a long list of options they have to figure out. Less, structured well, is more.

05

Defining the Right Scope Is Part of Good Design

We kept the MVP focused on the core decision flow. Every feature we cut made the remaining ones stronger. Scope isn't a constraint — it's a design choice.
